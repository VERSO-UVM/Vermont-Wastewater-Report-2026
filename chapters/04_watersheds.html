Wastewater and Watersheds#

This chapter examines the relationship between wastewater systems and Vermont’s watershed health.

Introduction#

Vermont’s wastewater systems discharge treated effluent into rivers, streams, and lakes. Understanding the interaction between wastewater treatment and watershed health is essential for:

  • Protecting water quality

  • Maintaining aquatic ecosystems

  • Ensuring compliance with water quality standards

  • Planning for nutrient management

Discharge Points and Receiving Waters#

Vermont’s active NPDES-permitted wastewater facilities (170 facilities as of January 2026) discharge treated effluent to a diverse array of receiving waters across the state. Understanding where facilities discharge and the cumulative loading to each waterbody is essential for watershed planning, TMDL implementation, and protecting designated uses.

Major Receiving Waters and Facility Counts#

Based on Vermont DEC’s active NPDES permit database, the following receiving waters accept discharges from the greatest number of facilities:

  • Winooski River: 19 facilities (including Burlington Main/North/River, Essex Junction, Waterbury, Montpelier, Richmond, Plainfield, Cabot, and tributaries like North Branch, Stevens Branch)

  • Connecticut River: 15 facilities (including Brattleboro, Bellows Falls, White River Junction, St. Johnsbury, Springfield, Canaan, and various hydroelectric facilities)

  • Otter Creek: 13 facilities (including Middlebury, Rutland, Proctor, Brandon, Vergennes, and industrial facilities)

  • Missisquoi River: 10 facilities (including Enosburg Falls, Richford, Swanton, Sheldon Springs, North Troy, Troy & Jay)

  • Lake Champlain: 10 facilities (including direct discharges from Burlington Main, St. Albans Water Treatment Plant, Swanton WTP, Grand Isle facilities, Champlain Water District, and various water treatment plants)

  • Lamoille River: 9 facilities (including Morrisville, Hardwick, Jeffersonville, Fairfax, Milton, and Johnson)

  • Black River: 4 facilities (including Ludlow, Springfield, Cavendish, and Irasburg NE Sand & Gravel)

  • Barton River: 2 facilities (Barton, Orleans)

  • Passumpsic River: 3 facilities (including St. Johnsbury, Lyndon, Weidmann Electrical Technology)

  • Deerfield River: 4 facilities (including Wilmington, Readsboro, hydroelectric, and resort facilities)

  • Ottauquechee River: 6 facilities (including Woodstock locations, Bridgewater, Hartford-Quechee, Killington)

  • White River: 4 facilities (including Bethel, Royalton, Chelsea, and tributaries)

  • Castleton River: 3 facilities (including Castleton, Fair Haven, West Rutland via Clarendon River)

  • Poultney River: 1 facility (Poultney)

  • Battenkill: 1 facility (Manchester)

  • Lake Memphremagog: 2 facilities (Newport City, Columbia Forest Products - wetlands contiguous)

  • Various tributaries, brooks, and streams: 60+ smaller facilities discharge to local unnamed tributaries, brooks (e.g., Potash Brook, Furnace Brook, Thatcher Brook, Kedron Brook, Sacketts Brook), wetlands, and bays

Geographic and Watershed Implications#

The concentration of facilities in the Winooski, Connecticut, and Lake Champlain basins reflects Vermont’s population centers and legacy of industrial/municipal development along river corridors. Otter Creek and Missisquoi River also carry significant point-source loads due to multiple upstream discharges.

Facilities discharging to Lake Champlain and its tributaries operate under especially stringent phosphorus limits to address TMDL requirements. Similarly, Connecticut River basin facilities must navigate interstate water quality coordination with New Hampshire and downstream states.

Understanding the spatial distribution of discharge points and the cumulative loading to shared receiving waters is critical for:

  • Assessing attainment of water quality standards

  • Allocating wasteload and load allocations under TMDLs

  • Evaluating impacts of new or expanded discharges

  • Prioritizing nutrient reduction strategies

  • Coordinating regional stormwater and CSO controls

Nutrient Loading#

Nutrient loading—particularly phosphorus and nitrogen—from wastewater treatment facilities represents a significant contributor to water quality impairments in Vermont’s surface waters. Excess nutrients drive eutrophication, harmful algal blooms, reduced dissolved oxygen, and impairment of aquatic habitat and recreational uses.

Phosphorus as the Primary Concern#

Phosphorus is the limiting nutrient in most Vermont surface waters, especially Lake Champlain and Lake Memphremagog. Point-source discharges from municipal and industrial wastewater facilities contribute measurable phosphorus loads that, combined with nonpoint sources (agriculture, stormwater, streambank erosion), exceed assimilative capacity in many watersheds. Vermont DEC has established Total Maximum Daily Loads (TMDLs) for phosphorus in the Lake Champlain basin and other impaired waters, resulting in progressively tighter effluent limits and technology upgrades (chemical precipitation, tertiary filtration) at WWTFs.

Nitrogen Loading and Downstream Impacts#

While phosphorus dominates lake concerns, nitrogen loading affects estuarine and downstream coastal waters (e.g., Long Island Sound via the Connecticut River), and elevated ammonia can cause toxicity to aquatic life. Some facilities face seasonal or year-round limits on total nitrogen, ammonia, or nitrate to protect cold-water fisheries and minimize contributions to downstream nitrogen-driven eutrophication.

Wasteload Allocations and Compliance#

Under TMDLs, wastewater facilities receive wasteload allocations (WLAs) expressed as concentration limits (mg/L) or mass-based limits (kg/yr or lbs/yr). Facilities in the Lake Champlain basin have seen WLAs reduced over multiple permit cycles, requiring capital upgrades, operational optimization, and enhanced monitoring. Indirect discharge systems must also demonstrate nutrient attenuation through soil treatment or report groundwater nutrient concentrations to prevent cumulative impacts on receiving streams.

Tracking and Reporting#

Facilities report monthly nutrient concentrations and calculate annual loads through discharge monitoring reports (DMRs). Vermont DEC compiles these data to track progress toward TMDL targets, assess cumulative basin-wide loading, and inform adaptive management strategies. Improved data quality, real-time monitoring, and optimization of chemical dosing help facilities maintain compliance while managing costs.

Stressor Identification and Management#

Vermont’s Watershed Management Division has identified nutrient enrichment as a priority stressor in multiple basins, with point sources contributing alongside nonpoint sources. Effective nutrient management requires coordinated strategies across sectors—upgrades at WWTFs, agricultural best practices, stormwater controls, and riparian restoration—to achieve water quality standards and restore impaired waters.

For detailed analysis of nutrient stressors and management strategies in Vermont watersheds, see Vermont DEC’s Non-Erosion Nutrients Stressor Plan.

Water Quality Impacts#

Wastewater discharges influence receiving water quality through multiple pathways—nutrient enrichment, oxygen demand, pathogen loading, thermal effects, and introduction of emerging contaminants. Understanding these impacts is essential for evaluating compliance with water quality standards, identifying impairments, and designing effective restoration strategies.

Nutrient Enrichment and Eutrophication#

Excessive phosphorus and nitrogen from wastewater discharges drive eutrophication in Vermont’s lakes and rivers, manifesting as algal blooms (including harmful cyanobacteria), reduced water clarity, taste and odor problems, and depletion of dissolved oxygen. Lake Champlain, Lake Memphremagog, and numerous smaller lakes and ponds are listed as impaired due to nutrient enrichment, with point-source contributions from WWTFs representing a measurable fraction of total loads. The Vermont Clean Water Initiative tracks progress in reducing phosphorus to meet TMDL targets and improve lake water quality.

Dissolved Oxygen and Aquatic Life#

Biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) from incompletely treated wastewater consumes dissolved oxygen in receiving waters, potentially stressing cold-water fisheries and other aquatic life. Vermont’s water quality standards establish minimum dissolved oxygen criteria (e.g., 6-7 mg/L for cold-water fisheries), and facilities are required to meet secondary treatment standards (typically 30/30 for BOD/TSS mg/L) or more stringent site-specific limits to protect downstream uses. Summer low-flow and high-temperature conditions exacerbate oxygen depletion risks.

Pathogen Loading and Recreation#

Treated effluent, CSO/SSO events, and inadequate onsite systems can introduce fecal coliform, E. coli, and other pathogens to surface waters, threatening public health and recreational uses (swimming, boating, fishing). Beach closures, no-contact advisories, and impairments for primary contact recreation are tracked by Vermont DEC. Disinfection (UV, chlorination) at WWTFs and CSO control investments aim to reduce pathogen loads and restore safe recreation.

Thermal Impacts#

Large WWTFs discharging to small or thermally sensitive streams can elevate water temperatures, stressing cold-water species like trout and impacting biological communities. Vermont’s thermal water quality criteria (temperature maximums and rate-of-change limits) apply to receiving waters, and some permits include temperature monitoring or seasonal discharge restrictions to protect cold-water habitat.

Emerging Contaminants#

Pharmaceuticals, personal care products, microplastics, and per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) pass through conventional wastewater treatment and enter surface waters. While Vermont does not yet have widespread effluent limits for these contaminants, monitoring and research are ongoing to assess occurrence, impacts, and potential treatment upgrades to address emerging concerns.

Cumulative and Synergistic Effects#

Multiple stressors—nutrients, low dissolved oxygen, temperature, pathogens, and contaminants—interact to degrade aquatic ecosystems. Waters receiving multiple discharges or combinations of point and nonpoint sources face cumulative impacts that may exceed assimilative capacity even when individual facilities meet permit limits. Watershed-scale assessments and adaptive management are necessary to address these complex interactions.

Monitoring and Assessment#

Vermont DEC conducts routine monitoring of receiving waters, including:

  • Fixed-station water quality monitoring: Long-term trends in nutrients, dissolved oxygen, temperature, and pH

  • Biological monitoring: Macroinvertebrate and fish community assessments to evaluate ecosystem health

  • Beach monitoring: E. coli sampling at public beaches and swimming areas

  • Lake and river assessments: Periodic surveys for phosphorus, chlorophyll-a, Secchi depth, and algal bloom occurrence

These data inform the biennial Integrated Water Quality Assessment (303(d)/305(b) Report), which identifies impaired waters, tracks restoration progress, and prioritizes TMDL development and implementation.

Vermont Clean Water Initiative#

The Vermont Clean Water Initiative 2025 Performance Report provides comprehensive tracking of state and federal clean water investments, regulatory program implementation, and progress toward Lake Champlain phosphorus TMDL targets. The report demonstrates how coordinated efforts across point-source controls, agricultural best practices, stormwater management, and natural resource restoration are making measurable improvements in water quality.
